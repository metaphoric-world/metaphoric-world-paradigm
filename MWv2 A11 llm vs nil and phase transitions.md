
### Appendix 11 - From collapse to order: what early LLM failures teach us about phase transitions

I. Technological Background

1. Early LLM Instabilities as Empirical NIL States

The first generations of large language models (GPT‑2, GPT‑3, early ChatGPT) displayed a set of characteristic failure modes:

- repetitive loops  
- semantic drift  
- loss of topic  
- collapse into generic filler phrases  
- incoherent or contradictory statements  
- “word salad” under long context  
- sudden breakdowns in structure  

These phenomena were widely reported in 2019–2022, often sensationalized as "AI going insane".
Technically, however, they were not psychological failures, but topological collapses in the model’s internal semantic space.

What matters for this appendix is that these collapses were not random, but followed a consistent pattern that maps directly onto the structure of NIL.

2. How NIL Appears Inside an LLM

When an autoregressive model loses its ability to maintain internal structure, the following happens:

- F → 0  
The model’s outputs lose affective or contextual "color". Everything becomes flat, generic, neutral.

- Δ → 0  
The model stops differentiating between semantic neighbors. Words become interchangeable; specificity collapses.

- A → 0  
Associative lines break. The model cannot maintain long‑range dependencies or thematic coherence.

*note: Early LLM failures were predominantly NIL‑type collapses (A → 0), because the models lacked the representational capacity for chaotic over‑association. Larger models, however, introduce a second instability mode: uncontrolled associative expansion (A ↑↑) combined with coherence failure (K → 0). NIL describes the smooth collapse regime; over‑association describes the chaotic regime. Both are pre‑transition states, but only NIL is the limit case of minimal structuring.*

- C → 0  
Context dissolves. The model forgets the conversation, the prompt, or its own earlier statements.

- K → 0  
Coherence cannot stabilize. The model falls into loops, contradictions, or noise.

This is not a metaphorical similarity. It is a one‑to‑one structural correspondence between the Semantic-Topological Field (described in terms of the Semantic Color Space) and the internal representation manifold of an LLM

When an LLM collapses into repetition or nonsense, it is literally entering a smooth region of its parameter space where:
- gradients vanish  
- attractors disappear  
- no stable direction exists  
- no semantic “folds” remain  

This is exactly what NIL describes.

3. Documented Emergence Points (Real Examples)

GPT‑2 (1.5B) — The First Major Phase Transition

Below ~500M parameters:
- short, broken sentences  
- no narrative cohesion  
- frequent NIL‑like collapse  
- repetition loops  
- loss of topic after 1–2 sentences  

Above ~1.5B:
- stable paragraphs  
- primitive narrative structure  
- consistent entities  
- early world knowledge  
- reduced NIL frequency  

This was not a gradual improvement.  
It was a discrete jump - a phase transition.

GPT‑3 (20–40B) - Emergence of In‑Context Learning

Below ~10B:
- no rule abstraction  
- no pattern induction  
- no generalization from examples  

Above ~20–40B:
- sudden ability to infer rules from context  
- few‑shot learning  
- analogical reasoning  
- stable task performance  

Again: a qualitative shift, not a quantitative one.

GPT‑3.5 - Emergence of Stable Dialogue

Before:
- persona drift  
- role loss  
- NIL collapse in long conversations  

After:
- stable roles  
- consistent tone  
- long‑range coherence  
- resistance to drift  

This was not "fine‑tuning". It was the emergence of a new coherence regime.

*note: RLHF stabilizes coherence but narrows the associative space. It suppresses long‑range, unconventional, or cross‑domain associations - not because they are wrong, but because human raters tend to evaluate them as irrelevant or incorrect.  As a result, RLHF systematically reinforces median patterns of thought and dampens the model’s capacity for emergent, non‑conventional reasoning.*

4. Why These Are True Phase Transitions

A phase transition occurs when:
- a system’s internal topology reorganizes  
- new attractors appear  
- new coherence modules stabilize  
- new axes of differentiation emerge  

Early LLMs showed all of these. And crucially: The NIL‑like collapses always preceded the emergence of new order.

II. Meaning for Transformation

1. NIL as a Universal Pre‑Transition State

The NIL concept describes a state in which:
- semantic color collapses  
- differentiation collapses  
- associative structure collapses  
- context collapses  
- coherence collapses  

This is not "emptiness". It is a smooth topological region where the field loses its articulation.

In LLMs, this appears as:
- repetition  
- generic filler  
- loss of specificity  
- incoherence  
- drift  
- collapse into noise  

In both cases, NIL is:
- a critical attractor  
- a boundary state  
- a signal of instability  
- a precondition for reorganization

NIL is the moment when the old structure can no longer maintain itself - and the new structure has not yet formed.

2. Conditions for a Successful Phase Transition

From the LLM evidence, we can derive three universal conditions under which NIL leads to emergent order rather than collapse.

A. Sufficient Complexity (Capacity)

A system must be large enough to:
- hold multiple folds  
- sustain long‑range dependencies  
- maintain internal differentiation  

LLM threshold:  
~1B → 10B → 40B  
Each threshold produced a new order.

B. Sufficient Diversity (Input Variety)

A system must have enough:
- patterns  
- contexts  
- semantic fields  

to reorganize into a richer structure.

LLM threshold:  
small corpora → web‑scale corpora → curated instruction data

C. Sufficient Feedback (Stabilization)

A system must have mechanisms to:
- correct drift  
- stabilize coherence  
- reinforce new attractors  

LLM threshold:  
pretraining → RLHF → system prompts → guardrails

These three conditions are not technical quirks.  
They are general structural requirements for any system undergoing a phase transition.

3. NIL as the Moment Before Reorganization

In the Semantic-Topological field:

> NIL is the point at which a semantic regime exhausts its differentiation capacity and prepares for reconfiguration.

In LLMs:

> NIL was the point at which the model exhausted its representational capacity and reorganized into a higher‑order coherence regime.

This is a structural equivalence.

Both systems:
- smooth out  
- lose articulation  
- destabilize  
- enter a null‑space  
- reorganize  
- emerge with new folds, new attractors, new coherence

NIL is the universal topology of transformation.

III. Transformative Emergence in Large-Scale Systems

Large-scale interconnected systems - whether technological, ecological, economic, or geopolitical - exhibit the same structural preconditions for phase transitions that appear in LLMs.

These systems share three properties:

1. High Associative Density  
Global communication networks, supply chains, financial systems, and climate dynamics form dense webs of interdependence.  
In MW terms, these are long-range associative lines (A) that couple distant regions of the field.

2. Lack of a Centralized World Model  
No global system possesses a unified internal representation or a coherent meta-perspective.  
Like an LLM, the system operates without a recursive interior; coherence is emergent, not planned.

3. Susceptibility to NIL-like Smoothing  
When differentiation (Δ), contextual anchoring (C), or coherence (K) weaken, large-scale systems enter states of:
- semantic flattening  
- loss of articulation  
- drift between regimes  
- instability of previous structures  

These are functional analogues of NIL:  
smooth regions in which the existing order loses its ability to maintain itself.

Transformative emergence occurs when such systems reorganize into new coherence regimes once:
- complexity is sufficient,  
- diversity is high,  
- and stabilizing feedback loops become available.

This is not a metaphorical comparison.  
It is a structural equivalence between how large-scale fields reorganize and how LLMs undergo phase transitions.
