

### Chapter 11 - Semantic-Topological forms of development

Introduction

Semantic fields do not remain static.  
They fold, harden, thin out, crystallize, or reorganize in response to the pressures acting upon them.  
Chapter 11 examines these developmental forms - not as cultural anomalies or linguistic curiosities, but as expressions of how the field organizes itself under different structural conditions.

The examples in this chapter span a wide range:
- transitional field‑states such as NIL  
- large‑scale cultural phenomena such as taboos  
- micro‑structural regularities of language such as adjective order  
- phonological attractors and rhythmic patterns  

Despite their diversity, they share a common logic:  
each represents a stable or metastable configuration of the semantic‑topological field.

Some of these configurations arise when the field is overloaded (as in archaic taboos), others when it becomes over‑hardened (as in modern crystalline taboos), and still others when it becomes over‑smoothed (as in NIL).  
Linguistic structures, too, can be understood as field‑solutions: ways in which the system minimizes instability and maximizes coherence under the constraints of communication.

In this sense, the developmental forms presented here are not exceptions but expressions of the field’s general dynamics.  
They show how meaning organizes itself when confronted with:
- high affective charge  
- low contextual permeability  
- collapsing associative lines  
- excessive smoothing  
- or the need for rapid, low‑entropy processing  

Chapter 11 therefore demonstrates that the same parameters - F, Δ, A, C, K - govern both the evolution of cultural structures and the micro‑patterns of language.

What follows are three families of developmental forms:
1. NIL — the limit case of minimal structuring  
2. Taboos — semantic no‑zones that regulate overload, over‑hardening, or smoothing  
3. Linguistic attractors — structural regularities such as adjective order and phonological patterns  

Together, they illustrate how the semantic field stabilizes itself, loses stability, or reorganizes into new configurations.  
They show that meaning is not a fixed property of expressions, but a dynamic topological process — one that continually reshapes itself across scales, from cultural history to the grammar of a single sentence.

### 11.1 - NIL – The Semantic Null‑Space

In many contemporary contexts, one can observe a subtle but pervasive thinning of meaning - a drift toward semantic neutrality, reduced resonance, and weakened contextual anchoring.  
This is not the result of individual decisions or cultural failure, but a field‑state: a configuration that emerges when long‑term structural tendencies converge toward minimal differentiation.

NIL designates this configuration.  
It is not an absence of meaning, but a semantic‑topological limit case: the state in which the structuring parameters of the field fall below a critical threshold.

11.1.1 - NIL as Boundary Case of the Semantic Color Space

The semantic color space is defined by five parameters (plus FTM):

- F - Color Value (affective and cultural charge)  
- Δ - Uncertainty Radius (local semantic differentiation)  
- A - Associative Lines (connective potential between elements)  
- C - Context (narrative, situational, or social embedding)  
- K - Coherence Module (emergent stability of the configuration)

An element carries meaning when these parameters exceed a minimal threshold.

NIL denotes the state in which they fall below that threshold:

- F → 0 (color value neutralizes)  
- Δ → 0 (uncertainty radius collapses)  
- A → 0 (associative lines break)  
- C → 0 (context dissolves)  
- K → 0 (coherence cannot stabilize)

NIL is therefore not “nothing,” but a null‑space that appears when the semantic topology loses its internal articulation.

11.1.2 - Topological Structure of NIL

Topologically, NIL is a smooth space:
- no folds  
- no gradients  
- no attractors  
- no contextual nodes  
- no triadic structures  

The semantic distance between all elements becomes effectively constant;  
no preferred directions, densities, or resonance paths remain.

NIL is the limit case of minimal structuring within the color space.

11.1.3 - NIL as Operator in the Field

NIL functions as:
- a critical attractor (when differentiation collapses)  
- a boundary state (when F, Δ, A, C fall below threshold)  
- an instability indicator (when K can no longer stabilize the configuration)

NIL is not a content, but a state that emerges when the structuring parameters of an element become undercritical.

11.1.4 - Derivation of NIL from Basis‑2 Dominance

The exponential strategy of Basis 2 is characterized by:
- doubling  
- scalability  
- efficiency maximization  
- binarization  

These mechanisms produce a systemic smoothing of semantic topology.  
Their effects are not theoretical; they can be observed in contemporary semantic environments.

11.1.4.1 - Doubling
→ reduces context (C ↓), lowers differentiation (Δ ↓)

Doubling creates copies that are functionally identical but contextually thinner than the original.  
Examples include:
- mass‑produced cultural forms (memes, templates, formats) that replicate without carrying their original contextual depth  
- repeated political or commercial slogans that lose semantic density through iteration  
- algorithmic content replication (e.g., reposting, rephrasing, summarization) that strips away local nuance  

Each iteration reduces Δ (uncertainty radius) and weakens C (context), producing a smoother semantic surface.

11.1.4.2 - Scalability
→ neutralizes color value (F ↓), favors standardization

Scalability requires forms that can circulate across contexts without friction.  
This demands neutral affective profiles and minimal cultural specificity.

Examples:
- globalized branding that avoids culturally charged symbols to remain universally legible  
- standardized interfaces, icons, and UX patterns that suppress local variation  
- simplified language in international communication (e.g., corporate English) that avoids idiom, metaphor, or emotional coloration  

Scalability therefore pushes F → 0, producing semantically pale, low‑contrast forms.

11.1.4.3 - Efficiency Maximization
→ eliminates ambiguity, weakens associative lines (A ↓)

Efficiency rewards forms that are:
- unambiguous  
- predictable  
- low‑entropy  
- easy to process  

This suppresses the rich associative networks that normally give meaning its depth.

Examples:
- preference for bullet‑point communication over narrative explanation (the author is aware of the irony of presenting this as a bullet point)
- algorithmic ranking systems that reduce complex judgments to single metrics  
- optimization logics (e.g., “best practices”) that eliminate alternative pathways  

Efficiency collapses A (associative lines), leaving only the shortest, most functional connections.

11.1.4.4 - Binarization
→ destabilizes contextual nodes (C ↓), smooths triadic structures (K cannot stabilize)

Binarization replaces continuous or triadic structures with oppositional categories.

Examples:
- public discourse structured around for/against, true/false, win/lose  
- algorithmic classification systems that force ambiguous cases into discrete bins  
- social media dynamics that amplify polarities and suppress intermediate positions  

Binarization erodes C (context) and prevents K (coherence module) from stabilizing complex configurations.

11.1.4.5 - Summary of Mechanisms

Together, these Basis‑2 mechanisms:
- reduce F (color value)  
- collapse Δ (uncertainty radius)  
- weaken A (associative lines)  
- dissolve C (context)  
- destabilize K (coherence)  

The result is a smooth semantic surface — the precondition for NIL.

11.1.5 - The Movement: Basis 2 → Smoothing → NIL

The mechanisms of Basis 2 lead to:

1. smoothing of differentiation structures (Δ → 0)  
2. reduction of F, Δ, A, C  
3. collapse of K (coherence cannot form)  
4. transition into the NIL state  

NIL is thus a necessary consequence of the exponential strategy - not an endpoint, but a critical transitional configuration.

11.1.6 - NIL as Indicator of an Impending Phase Shift

NIL marks the moment when a semantic regime:
- loses internal coherence (K → 0)  
- exhausts its differentiation capacity (Δ → 0)  
- destabilizes its associative lines (A → 0)  
- decouples its contextual nodes (C → 0)  

This state is not stable. It is the threshold of a reorganization of the semantic‑topological field.

In the field model:
> NIL is the critical point at which a global phase shift is prepared —  
> a reconfiguration that generates new folds, new resonance spaces, and new axes of differentiation.

NIL is not cultural collapse, but a signal of transformation.

11.1.7 - NIL as Boundary Case of the Axioms

From the axioms:
- the possibility field = space of potentiality  
- distinction = mechanism of structuring  

NIL is the state in which:
- the possibility field becomes under‑structured  
- distinctions cease to be effective  

It is the ontological limit case of minimal structuring.

11.1.8 - NIL in the Basis‑2 / Basis‑3 Framework

- Basis 3 generates folds, resonance spaces, triadic structures  
- Basis 2 generates smoothing, reduction, binarization  

NIL is the state in which:
- Basis‑3 structures have been widely displaced  
- Basis‑2 structures have homogenized the field  
- a phase shift is imminent

11.1.9 - Summary

NIL is:
- a null‑space of the semantic field  
- a critical attractor  
- an indicator of instability  
- the limit case of minimal structuring  
- the systemic consequence of Basis‑2 dominance  
- the threshold of a global phase transition  

NIL is not the end of meaning,  
but the point at which a new semantic regime becomes possible.

### 11.2 - Taboos as Semantic No‑Zones

Archaic, Religious, and Modern Taboos in the Color‑Space Model

In the semantic color‑space, a taboo is not an empty region but a restricted zone, characterized by:
- a high uncertainty radius (Δ)  
- high color value (F)  
- low context permeability (C → elements cannot shift context without generating instability)  
- disrupted or severed associative lines (A) 
- coherence modules (K) that fail to integrate the configuration, resulting in a locally blocked region of the field

Taboos are (metaphorically speaking) oxygen‑poor regions of the meaning field: places where semantic breathing is not possible.  
(Note: taboos share a functional role but not a fixed parameter-signature.)

Their historical development can be traced precisely through shifts in cultural base‑logics - especially the growing dominance of Basis 2 and the resulting spread of NIL (semantic smoothing and loss of differentiation).

11.2.1 - Archaic Taboo – The Autopoietic Restriction Field

The archaic taboo does not arise from deliberate regulation but from collective semantic self‑organization. It marks those regions where the meaning field reaches its symbolic load‑bearing limit.

What does the archaic taboo protect against?
“Unspeakable” does not mean that phenomena such as death, birth, or sexuality could not be named, but that in archaic cultures they were experienced as threshold events that overload the symbolic order:
- transitions between ontological domains  
- experiences that cannot be fully symbolized  
- situations in which individual and collective coherence becomes fragile  

The taboo does not protect against the event itself,  
but against the semantic destabilization such transitions can trigger. But who or what is protected exactly?
- the individual → from psychological overload  
- the community → from semantic fragmentation  
- the worldview → from dissolution of the symbolic order  

It is realized through symbolic practices:
- rituals → controlled encirclement  
- shamans → semantic boundary‑crossers  
- myths → narrative stabilization  

The archaic taboo is autopoietic:it emerges from collective experience, not from planning.

Color‑Space Form
- F: extremely high  
- Δ: extremely high  
- C: absent  
- A: circular or interrupted  
- K: fails to integrate → the field registers a blocked zone

11.2.2 - Religiously Codified Taboo - The Structured Restriction Field

With the rise of more complex religions, taboos become formalized. They are no longer merely avoided but embedded ritually, narratively, and doctrinally.
This transformation through Cultural Structuring encompasses the following aspects:
- taboos become contextualized (purity, impurity, holiness)  
- they acquire rules, commandments, liturgical forms  
- they become functionalized (social order, identity, boundary‑making)

Color‑Space Form
- F: high, but differentiated  
- Δ: deliberately constrained  
- C: emerges (myths, commandments, liturgy)  
- A: channeled  
- K: recognizes taboos as stabilizing elements  

Religion becomes a semantic architecture that does not dissolve taboos but embeds them.

11.2.3 - Modern Crystalline Taboos - Restriction Zones in Basis‑2 Cultures

Modernity is the first epoch in which Basis 2 - the principle of binary, scalable, efficiency‑oriented differentiation - becomes culturally dominant.  
Basis 2 systematically produces:
- smoothing  
- homogenization  
- loss of context  
- loss of resonance  
- dilution of meaning  
→ NIL spreads.

Modern taboos do not arise from reverence but from semantic over‑hardening. They are responses to the structural tensions generated by Basis 2.

Two subtypes can be distinguished.

11.2.3.1 - Explicitly Codified Modern Taboos
(e.g., Holocaust denial, racist slurs)

Properties
- F: extremely high  
- Δ: near zero (maximally fixed)  
- C: strictly regulated (legal, moral)  
- A: severed or permitted only in meta‑discourse  
- K: responds with immediate blocking  

These taboos function like crystal nuclei that locally freeze the meaning field. They do not protect against overload but against systemic rupture.

11.2.3.2 - Implicit Structural Modern Taboos
(e.g., criticism of meritocracy, questioning state legitimacy, critique of economic axioms)

Properties
- F: medium‑high but diffuse  
- Δ: semantically wide, discursively narrow  
- C: present but ideologically over‑coded  
- A: seemingly open, actually channeled  
- K: responds with redirection rather than blocking  

These taboos structure discourse through non‑thematisation. Their effect is subtle but systemically significant.

11.2.4 - Basis 2 as NIL‑Generator - and the Role of the Taboo

Modern taboo formation can be understood only against the background of Basis 2, whose logic of binarization, scalability, and efficiency gradually reshapes the semantic field.  
Where Basis 3 produces folds, resonance, and contextual depth, Basis 2 tends toward smoothing: it reduces variation, compresses transitions, and favors forms that circulate without friction.  
NIL is the limit state of this trajectory — the point at which the field loses differentiation energy.

11.2.4.1 - Why Taboos Crystallize in Basis‑2 Cultures

In such an environment, taboos take on a new function. Where Basis‑3 cultures rely on resonance and contextual embedding to stabilize meaning, Basis‑2 cultures lack these resources.  
Taboos therefore become semantic emergency architectures: local hardenings that compensate for the field’s general tendency toward smoothing.

They artificially keep F high, fix Δ at narrow values, stabilize C through strict regulation, sever A to prevent drift, and relieve K by providing rigid boundaries where the field would otherwise dissolve.  
Modern taboos are thus not expressions of reverence but countermeasures against the systemic pressures of Basis 2.

11.2.4.2 - Historical Synthesis

Viewed historically, taboos trace the evolution of cultural base‑logics:

| Epoch | Dominant Basis | Taboo Type | Function |
|-------|----------------|------------|----------|
| Archaic | Basis 3 | autopoietic | protection from overload |
| Religion | 2 → 3 mixed | codified | protection of order |
| Modernity | Basis 2 | crystalline | protection from system rupture / NIL |

Taboos emerge wherever the meaning field reaches its limits - whether through overload (Basis 3) or over‑hardening (Basis 2).

11.2.4.3 - Taboos as Topological Regulators

From a topological perspective, taboos are not moral artifacts but regulators of field stability.  
They limit drift, stabilize coherence, protect cultural orders, and prevent both semantic overload and semantic smoothing.  
They function as pressure valves in systems shaped by Basis 3 as well as Basis 2, and thus serve as precise indicators of the dominant base‑logic of an epoch.

11.2.5 - Late‑Modern Taboo Erosion - NIL as Phase Transition

11.2.5.1 - Crystalline Taboos Require a Crystalline Field

Modern taboos - e.g. Holocaust denial, racist slurs, criticism of state legitimacy or meritocratic axioms - depend on a field in which meanings are sharply defined, discourses clearly structured, moral markers strongly coded, sanctions effective, and context nodes stable.  
They presuppose a semantic crystal lattice.  
When this lattice softens, the taboos soften with it.

11.2.5.2 - How NIL Undermines Crystalline Taboos

NIL dismantles the very conditions that once stabilized modern taboos.  
As F declines, words lose depth and taboos lose affective charge.  
As C erodes, discourses lose structure and taboos lose their embedding.  
As A smooths, transitions become arbitrary and taboos lose their boundaries.  
As K distorts, either everything becomes equally important or nothing holds together, and taboos lose both priority and orientation.  
As Δ becomes chaotic, meanings drift without forming new structures, and taboos lose contour.

In short:
> NIL consumes the semantic energy that once made taboos effective.

11.2.5.3 - Why Taboos Feel “Still There, but Weakened”

This duality - persistence without force - is characteristic of NIL.  
Taboos remain legally, morally, institutionally, and rhetorically present, yet they lose affective intensity, cultural resonance, social enforceability, symbolic depth, and discursive clarity.  
The shell remains; the core erodes.

11.2.5.4 - Absorption Rather Than Destruction

NIL does not attack taboos; it absorbs them.  
As the field smooths conflicts, meanings, values, identities, and narratives, taboos are not broken but hollowed out. They lose sharpness, function, and cultural necessity, becoming semantic fossils: still visible, but no longer alive.

The trajectory can be modeled in three phases:
1. Crystallization (Basis 2 dominates): taboos are sharp and highly charged.  
2. Over‑structuring (Basis 2 over‑hardens): taboos become politicized, moralized, legally fixed.  
3. NIL absorption (Basis 2 collapses into smoothing): taboos lose F, C, A, and K; they persist but cease to function.

11.2.5.5 - Why This Is So Strongly Felt Today

Digital communication radicalizes Basis 2; algorithmic normalization destroys context; information overload dilutes F; meme culture smooths A; permanent outrage overloads K; ironic distance destabilizes Δ.  
The result is a cultural field so flat that even strong taboos sink into it.

11.2.5.6 - Synthesis and Outlook 

NIL as the Endpoint of Modern Taboo Evolution

| Epoch | Basis | Taboo Type | Condition |
|-------|-------|------------|-----------|
| Archaic | Basis 3 | autopoietic | alive, dangerous |
| Religion | 2→3 | codified | stable, functional |
| Modernity | Basis 2 | crystalline | sharp, highly charged |
| Late Modernity | Basis 2 → NIL | hollowed | formal, but weak |

The weakening of modern taboos is not accidental but the direct consequence of Basis 2’s dominance and the spread of NIL. Taboos do not disappear; they lose their semantic energy. They remain as structures but lose their cultural force.

The erosion of crystalline taboos signals not cultural decline but a phase transition in the meaning field. As the semantic crystal lattice loses tension, it creates space for new orders.  
NIL is not an endpoint but an intermediate climate -  a semantic interregnum in which new context nodes, associative lines, and coherences can form.  
After periods of over‑hardening and flattening, cultural systems often reorganize into triadic, rhythmic, or context‑rich patterns — not as regression, but as reconfiguration.

### 11.3 - Linguistic Attractors: Adjective Ordering as Field Dynamics

Natural language is not a purely symbolic system.  
It is a hybrid phenomenon: a continuous semantic field that periodically crystallizes into discrete units.  
This dual nature means that linguistic structures often emerge not from rules, but from field‑stability constraints.  
One of the clearest examples is the English ordering of attributive adjectives.

Native speakers follow a remarkably stable sequence:

> Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Color → Origin → Material → Purpose

This ordering is not taught, not consciously known, and not derivable from truth‑conditions or syntax.  
Yet it is felt as immediately “right”, and violations are perceived as degraded or even nonsensical.

The stability of this pattern is best understood not as a grammatical convention, but as the result of semantic–topological gradients that guide how modifiers approach the conceptual core of a noun.

11.3.1 - Adjectives as Vectors in the Semantic Field

Every adjective can be represented as a vector in the semantic field, characterized by three interdependent parameters:

F — Affective / cultural coloring
The degree to which an adjective carries subjective, emotional, or culturally loaded meaning.  
High F → interpretive, evaluative, context‑sensitive.

Δ — Semantic uncertainty radius
The range of variation an adjective exhibits across contexts.  
High Δ → flexible, unstable, open to reinterpretation.

C — Core proximity
The degree to which an adjective is tied to the object’s identity.  
High C → definitional, low variability, low interpretive load.

*Note: Here, C instantiates as core proximity - the degree to which an adjective is bound to the object's conceptual identity.*
*This is the local form context takes when the question is not situational embedding but structural distance from the noun's core.*

These parameters are not independent.  
They are projections of deeper macro‑axes that structure the entire field of language.

11.3.2 - The Macro‑Axes of Language

Language oscillates between two poles:

1. Ur‑sound → Differentiation  
   The drift from pre‑categorical resonance (rhythm, prosody, affective sound) toward discrete lexical units.

2. Resonance balancing → Cultural folding  
   The accumulation of metaphorical, historical, and cultural layers that stabilize meanings over time.

These axes are not linguistic inventions; they are structural consequences of how meaning emerges from the field.

- Near the Ur‑sound pole, expressions are affectively colored, fluid, and context‑dependent.  
- Near the differentiated pole, expressions are stable, low‑Δ, and tightly coupled to conceptual cores.  
- Cultural folding gradually pushes certain meanings toward stability, while others remain resonant and interpretive.

The micro‑parameters used for adjectives—F, Δ, C—are simply projections of these macro‑axes.  
They are not analytic additions; they are the local manifestations of the field’s global structure.

11.3.3 - Mapping Adjective Classes into the Field

The traditional adjective classes align naturally with the three parameters:

- Opinion adjectives (lovely, horrible)  
  → very high F, high Δ, low C  
- Size (little, enormous)  
  → moderately high F, moderate Δ  
- Age (old, new)  
  → moderate F, moderate to low Δ  
- Shape (round, square)  
  → low F, low Δ  
- Color (red, blue)  
  → low F, low Δ, perceptually grounded  
- Origin (French, African)  
  → culturally marked but categorically stable  
- Material (wooden, metal)  
  → minimal F, very low Δ, definitional  
- Purpose (sleeping [bag], running [shoes])  
  → functional core

Arranged along the gradient from peripheral to core‑proximal, they yield exactly the canonical ordering.

This is not a coincidence.  
It is the unique monotonic path through the field that minimizes turbulence.

11.3.4 - Field Stability and the Emergence of Ordering

A sequence of adjectives is a trajectory through the semantic field.  
Stable sequences follow a monotonic gradient:

- decreasing F  
- decreasing Δ  
- increasing C

This produces a smooth approach toward the noun’s conceptual core.

Violations force the trajectory to oscillate between distant regions of the field.  
This introduces:

- semantic turbulence  
- increased interpretive load  
- reduced coherence  
- perceptual markedness

Speakers do not reject such sequences because of a memorized rule, but because the field configuration becomes unstable.

11.3.5 - Implications

1. Grammar as emergent structure  
   Adjective ordering is not a syntactic rule but a field‑stability phenomenon.

2. Intuitive competence as field sensitivity  
   Speakers track gradients implicitly, not explicitly.

3. Cross‑domain explanatory power  
   The same field principles that explain cultural phenomena also explain micro‑linguistic structure.

4. Testability  
   Manipulating F or Δ experimentally should shift ordering preferences.

### 11.4 - Phonological Attractors: Extending the Model

The semantic–topological model does not stop at meaning.  
Because language is a hybrid of field and discrete structure, the same principles apply to phonology.  
Phonotactic regularities—traditionally explained through “markedness”—can be reinterpreted as field‑stability constraints.

This avoids the classical tautology:

> “Segment X is marked because it does not occur, and it does not occur because it is marked.”

Instead, segments are vectors in a phonological field with their own projections of F, Δ, and C.

11.4.1 - From Semantic Fields to Phonological Fields

For any segment in a language, we can define:

F_phon — Phonological salience
How noticeable, foreign, or socially marked a sound is.

Δ_phon — Variability of realization
How much the sound changes across contexts; how unstable or allophonically dispersed it is.

C_phon — Proximity to the phonological core
How central the sound is to the language’s syllable structure; how frequent and productive it is.

These parameters are not arbitrary.  
They are projections of the same macro‑axes:

- High Fphon → high Δphon → low C_phon  
- Low Fphon → low Δphon → high C_phon

This is the phonological analogue of the semantic dependencies.

11.4.2 - Positional Attractors in the Phonological Field

Just as nouns act as attractors for adjective sequences, phonotactic positions act as attractors for segments:

- syllable onset  
- syllable coda  
- word‑final position  
- stressed vs. unstressed positions

Each position corresponds to a region of the phonological field with its own stability constraints.

Example:  
The English word‑final coda favors segments with:

- low F_phon  
- low Δ_phon  
- high C_phon

This is why /t, k, p, s, n, l/ are stable in codas.

11.4.3 - The Case of [ts]

Traditional account:  
“English lacks word‑final [ts] because [ts] is marked.”

Topological account:

- F_phon high: [ts] is salient, mostly appearing in loanwords (pizza, tsunami).  
- Δ_phon high: limited allophonic variation; context‑restricted.  
- C_phon low: not part of the English phonological core; rare in high‑frequency vocabulary.

The English word‑final attractor prefers:

- low salience  
- low variability  
- high core proximity

[ts] does not match this attractor.  
Therefore it is unstable in word‑final position — not because it is “marked”, but because its parameter configuration is incompatible with the attractor.

This is a genuine explanation, not a reformulated tautology.

11.4.3.1 - A note on markedness

Classical phonology explains many distributional asymmetries through the notion of markedness. In its traditional formulation, an opposition consists of an unmarked member—frequent, simple, broadly distributed—and a marked member—rarer, more complex, restricted in use. Over time, markedness accumulated a wide range of diagnostic criteria: typological rarity, articulatory difficulty, late acquisition, perceptual instability, limited distribution, and social salience.

Most of these criteria, however, are descriptive rather than explanatory.  
They tend to collapse into circular statements:

- a sound is marked because it is rare,  
- and rare because it is marked;  
- marked because it is restricted,  
- and restricted because it is marked.

Such formulations restate the empirical pattern rather than accounting for it.  
The only genuinely non‑circular component of classical markedness theory is morphological marking: the presence of additional morphological material (e.g., plural cat‑s vs. singular cat). This is a real structural asymmetry, not inferred from distribution.

In phonology, however, markedness typically functions as a label applied after observing a distribution, not as a mechanism that predicts it. The semantic–topological model replaces this label with independently defined parameters—Fphon, Δphon, and C_phon—whose values are determined by the segment’s position in the phonological field, not by its frequency or distribution. A segment is dispreferred in a given position not because it is “marked”, but because its parameter configuration does not match the positional attractor.

This shift transforms markedness from a descriptive tag into a structural explanation grounded in the dynamics of the field.

11.4.4 - Predictive Leverage

Because the parameters are structural, the model predicts where [ts] should be tolerated:

- intervocalically (pizza)  
- in loanwords  
- in expressive or stylized forms

These predictions match actual usage.

11.4.5 - Unifying Adjective Ordering and Phonotactics

The extension reveals a shared architecture:

- adjectives and segments are both vectors in a field  
- both interact with positional attractors  
- both form stable sequences when following monotonic gradients  
- both produce turbulence when gradients are violated  
- both rely on the same macro‑axes of language

Thus, semantic and phonological regularities emerge from shared topological dynamics, not from domain‑specific rules.
