
### Chapter 14 - Early World‑Formation: The First Folds of the Field

A note on scope

The following chapter uses early childhood as an accessible window into world‑formation.  
The processes described here - primitive distinctions, proto‑metaphorical projections, the first stabilisations of form - begin long before the stage of a speaking child.  

A full account of world‑formation across developmental plateaus, including the emergence of the self as a world node, appears in Chapter 15. 
Likewise, metaphor has already been defined in Section II as a field operation: a projection between distinctions. Language is simply its first explicit form.  

This chapter therefore does not present the origin of metaphor or the origin of the self.  
It offers a didactic entry point: a phase in which the underlying structures of world‑formation become visible.

### 14.1 - The first distinctions

Before a child speaks, before it recognises objects, before it can name anything at all, it already performs the fundamental act from which all world‑formation arises:

> It draws distinctions.

These distinctions are not conceptual.  
They are enacted through the body:
- a shift of attention creates figure and background  
- reaching creates near and far  
- grasping creates stable and unstable  
- repetition creates same and not‑same

Each distinction is a small fold in the possibility‑field. It stabilises a pattern long enough for it to be encountered again.  
World‑formation begins as the emergence of local coherence.

Nothing here requires language.  
Nothing here requires a self.  
The field is simply beginning to organise itself through embodied differentiation.

### 14.2 - Proto‑metaphor: structure before language

Because distinctions can be related, they can also be projected onto one another.  
This is the earliest form of metaphor - not linguistic, not symbolic, but structural.

A rhythm in movement can align with a rhythm in sound.  
A pattern of approach can align with a pattern of expectation.  
A contour in vision can align with a contour in touch.

These alignments are not learned.  
They arise because the field seeks resonance:  
patterns stabilise when they can be mapped onto other patterns without tearing the topology that holds them.

This is proto‑metaphor:
- a projection between early distinctions  
- a transfer of structure  
- a first step toward relationality  
- the seed of Basis‑3

It is the field exploring itself through alignment.

### 14.3 - Folds, stabilisations, and emerging coherence

As distinctions repeat and proto‑metaphors accumulate, the field begins to stabilise:
- recurring patterns become attractors  
- repeated actions become loops  
- familiar configurations become proto‑objects  
- stable contrasts become proto‑categories

These are not yet concepts.  
They are coherence‑forms: local regions of the field that hold together long enough to be recognised.

World‑formation at this stage is not representational.  
It is performative.  
The child does not “learn the world”; it enacts it.
This means the child does not build a model of the world and then act on it; it enacts distinctions that constitute the world as it goes."

### 14.4 - Language as explicit metaphor

When language enters, it does not introduce metaphor - it reveals it.

Words make distinctions portable.  
They allow a pattern that was once tied to a situation to be invoked elsewhere.  
They turn implicit projections into explicit ones.

This is why early language is saturated with spatial and topological expressions - not because children are being poetic, but because the field is already organised through embodied distinctions:

- “up” / “down”  
- “in” / “out”  
- “on” / “off”  
- “together” / “apart”  
- “again” (pattern repetition)  
- “gone” (boundary crossing)  
- “more” (continuation of a trajectory)  
- “all done” (closure of a loop)

These are not stylistic choices.  
They are the linguistic surface of a deeper structural logic:  
the mind is already organising experience topologically, and language simply gives these structures a public form.

Language is therefore not the origin of metaphor, but its first visible articulation.

### 14.5 - Approaching the emergence of the self

In these early folds, the groundwork for the self is laid - but the self is not yet present as a stable centre.  
What emerges here is the precondition for a world‑node:
- stable distinctions  
- recurrent patterns  
- proto‑relations  
- the first sense of continuity  
- the first sense of agency as influence

These structures do not depend on language.  
World‑formation is already underway long before verbal expression appears; language merely exposes patterns that were previously enacted silently.

Only later - through further plateaus of world‑formation - will these structures consolidate into a durable locus of self‑maintenance, a centre of projection that can recognise itself as such.  
The child’s use of “I” does not create this centre; it is the first explicit sign that the centre has begun to stabilise.
