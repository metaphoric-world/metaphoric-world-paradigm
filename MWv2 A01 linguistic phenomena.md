
### Appendix 1 - Three linguistic phenomena

I - Linguistic field‑structures and cultural time

1. Framing the question

Languages differ not only in grammar, lexicon, or metaphor, but in the field‑structures through which they project experience.  
If subjective time is a Basis‑3 phenomenon - an affective, relational, context‑dependent modulation of experience - then spoken languages should reveal structural correspondences between:

- their form‑regions in the 27‑region topology,  
- their attractor profiles (π, φ, e, c),  
- and the temporal fields characteristic of the cultures that speak them.

The goal of this appendix is not to claim that languages determine time perception.  
Rather:

> Languages and temporal experience are parallel projections of the same underlying field.  
>  
> Where a language clusters in the topological space, its temporal phenomenology tends to cluster as well.

This allows us to compare languages not through metaphors or grammar, but through their field‑geometry.

2. Why classical linguistic studies found so little

Most cross‑linguistic research on time has focused on:

- lexical metaphors ("future ahead/behind"),  
- grammatical tense systems,  
- spatial mappings of time,  
- or written representations.

These approaches share two limitations:

(1) They measure Basis‑2 projections, not Basis‑3 fields.
Lexical metaphors, tense morphology, and written conventions are symbolic artifacts.  
They reveal how a language encodes time after it has been linearized — not how time is experienced.

(2) They ignore the continuous operators (F, Δ, A, C, K).
Subtle differences in:

- relevance (F),  
- differentiation radius (Δ),  
- associative structure (A),  
- contextual dependence (C),  
- and coherence (K)

are far more predictive of temporal phenomenology than lexicon or grammar.

(3) They treat languages as lists of features, not as fields.
Spoken languages are dynamic Basis‑3 fields with partial Basis‑2 linearization.  
Classical methods flatten them into discrete categories and miss the underlying geometry.

As a result, the empirical record appears weak or contradictory - not because differences are absent, but because the wrong level of structure was examined.

3. The MW methodology 

The Metaphoric World provides a field‑theoretic method for analyzing languages:

(1) Form‑Region Mapping (27‑Region Topology)
Each language exhibits characteristic tendencies along the three axes:

- identity (+/0/–) - How stable are roles, participants, reference points?
- difference (+/0/–) - How finely does the language segment change, time, and events?
- relation (+/0/–) - How explicit, linear, or contextual are connections between clauses/events?

These tendencies place the language in a region cluster of the 27‑region space.

(2) Attractor Profiling (π, φ, e, c)
Languages differ in how strongly they weight:

- π (cyclicity),  
- φ (emergent proportion),  
- e (process and drift),  
- c (locality and linear projection).

These attractors shape how time is projected in speech.

(3) Field Correspondence
Because both language and temporal experience are Basis‑3 fields, their attractor profiles tend to align:

> A π‑dominant language correlates with cyclical temporal experience.  
A c‑dominant language correlates with linear temporal experience.  
An e‑dominant language correlates with processual temporal experience.

This is not determinism - it is field isomorphy.

II - Phonological field convergence: The case of African English

1. The Empirical Puzzle

Across sub‑Saharan Africa, English is spoken by communities whose first languages belong to entirely different families:
- Niger‑Congo (e.g., Yoruba, Igbo, Zulu)  
- Nilo‑Saharan (e.g., Luo, Maasai)  
- Afro‑Asiatic (e.g., Amharic, Hausa)  
- Khoisan families  
- and dozens of isolates and micro‑families  

These languages differ dramatically in:
- phoneme inventories,  
- syllable structure,  
- prosodic systems,  
- tone vs. stress,  
- morphophonology.

Yet English spoken across these regions exhibits a recognisably similar accent profile.  
This similarity is not genealogical, not ethnic, and not reducible to "African features". 
It is a field‑structural phenomenon.

The question is:
> Why do typologically diverse L1 systems converge on a similar L2 English accent?

2. Why classical explanations fall short

Traditional accounts rely on:
- "substrate influence", 
- "transfer",  
- "shared features",  
- or vague notions of "African phonology".

These explanations fail for three reasons:

(1) They treat languages as lists of features, not as fields.
They assume that if L1s differ, their L2 outputs must differ — which is empirically false.

(2) They ignore parameter regimes.
Phonological systems can be typologically different yet share similar parameter profiles (e.g., CV‑dominance, tone, low vowel reduction).

(3) They lack a model of convergence.
They cannot explain why heterogeneous inputs produce similar outputs when mapped into the same L2 target.

The MW architecture, by contrast, is explicitly built to model convergence in attractor fields.

3. The MW interpretation: African English as a phonological attractor state

The MW implementation architecture provides three tools that map directly onto phonology:

- F - salience / prominence of features  
- Δ - variability / tolerance for deviation  
- C - proximity to a structural core or attractor  

These operators apply not only to semantic fields but to phonological fields as well.

3.1 - L1 Systems as parameter clusters

Many sub‑Saharan languages, despite being genealogically unrelated, share similar phonological parameter regimes:
- CV‑dominant syllable structure (high C‑proximity to open syllables)  
- limited consonant clusters (low Δ for complex onsets/codas)  
- stable, unreduced vowels (high F for full vowel quality)  
- tone or pitch‑accent systems (F concentrated in pitch, not stress)  
- syllable‑timed rhythm (C anchored in regular syllabic pulses)

In MW terms, these languages occupy adjacent regions in the phonological field:
- Identity: 0/+ (syllable‑based, not stress‑based)  
- Difference: 0 (low tolerance for cluster variation)  
- Relation: + (strong prosodic coherence)

3.2 - English as a phonological attractor

English, by contrast, has a very different attractor configuration:
- stress‑timed rhythm  
- heavy vowel reduction (schwa as central attractor)  
- complex consonant clusters  
- high Δ in vowel quality  
- prosodic prominence on stress, not tone

This creates a strong attractor basin that incoming L1 systems must adapt to.

3.3 - Convergence through shared parameter profiles

When L1 systems with similar F–Δ–C profiles are projected into the same L2 attractor, the MW architecture predicts:

> Convergent trajectories → similar emergent output.

This is exactly what we observe:
- cluster simplification  
- reduced vowel reduction  
- syllable‑timed rhythm  
- tonal or pitch‑driven intonation contours  
- stable vowel qualities  
- prosodic regularity over stress‑driven reduction

These are not "African traits". 
They are field‑responses of similar L1 parameter regimes encountering the same L2 attractor.

4. What "African English" Really Is

From an MW perspective:
> African English is not an identity marker.  
> It is a phonological attractor state.

It emerges because:
- L1 systems share similar phonological parameter configurations,  
- English provides a strong, cluster‑rich, reduction‑heavy attractor,  
- and the adaptation trajectories converge in the same region of the phonological field.

This produces a recognisable accent profile, even though the underlying L1s are unrelated.

The phenomenon is thus:
- areal, not ethnic  
- structural, not cultural  
- field‑based, not genealogical

And it demonstrates that MW’s architecture - originally developed for semantic fields - applies equally well to phonological convergence.

III – Field compatibility and accent difficulty in L2 acquisition

1. The problem: Why some L2 accents are harder than others

A long‑standing question in phonology and second‑language acquisition is why speakers of one language find certain L2 phonologies "easy" to approximate and others "nearly impossible", even when the L2s are typologically unrelated.

Classical explanations rely on:
- segmental inventories,  
- phonotactic constraints,  
- markedness hierarchies,  
- or “transfer” from L1 to L2.

These approaches correctly identify local factors but fail to explain the global pattern:
> Accent difficulty is not proportional to linguistic distance.  
It is proportional to field‑compatibility.

This is precisely the kind of phenomenon the Metaphoric World architecture is designed to model.

2. Why classical accounts fail

Traditional L2 phonology research struggles for three reasons:

(1) It treats phonology as a list of features, not a field.
Segment inventories and phonotactic rules are discrete descriptions.  
They do not capture the continuous attractor dynamics that shape actual speech production.

(2) It lacks a model of parameter gradients.
Languages differ not only in what sounds they have, but in:

- F - salience and prominence of contrasts  
- Δ - variability and contextual modulation  
- C - proximity to stable phonological cores  

These parameters determine how easily an L1 system can adapt to an L2 attractor.

(3) It cannot explain asymmetric difficulty.
Why is French hard for Japanese speakers but Japanese not hard for French speakers?  
Why is Russian easier for Japanese speakers than French, despite being typologically “further away”?

Classical models have no answer.  
MW does.

3. The MW method: L2 acquisition as field transition

The MW implementation architecture treats phonology as a Basis‑3 field with partial Basis‑2 linearization.  
Accent difficulty is modeled as a transition from one parameter regime to another.

Three operators are central:
- F - Salience  
- Δ - Variability  
- C - Core‑Proximity  

These interact as follows:
> High F → high Δ → low C  
> Low F → low Δ → high C

This means:
- high‑F systems require flexible, context‑sensitive articulation  
- high‑C systems require stable, low‑variability articulation  
- transitions between regimes can be smooth or turbulent depending on compatibility

Accent difficulty is therefore:
> the turbulence generated when an L1 parameter regime enters an L2 attractor with incompatible F–Δ–C gradients.

4. Case study: Japanese L1 → French vs. Russian L2

This section demonstrates the MW logic with a concrete example.

4.1 - The L1 Parameter Regime: Japanese

Japanese phonology is:
- low F (few salient contrasts)  
- low Δ (low contextual variability)  
- high C (extremely stable CV‑based core)  

In MW terms:

> Japanese = low‑F, low‑Δ, high‑C attractor.

4.2 - The L2 attractor: French

French phonology is:
- high F (nasal vowels, rounded front vowels, schwa alternations)  
- high Δ (massive context‑dependent variation)  
- low C (unstable vowel cores, liaison, reduction)  

Thus:
> French = high‑F, high‑Δ, low‑C attractor.

4.3 - The L2 Attractor: Russian

Russian phonology is:
- medium F (palatalization, but fewer vowel qualities)  
- medium Δ (vowel reduction, but less extreme than French)  
- medium C (robust consonant system, stable cores)  

Thus:
> Russian = mid‑F, mid‑Δ, mid‑C attractor.

5. MW‑internal prediction: French is much harder than Russian

Now apply the transition logic:

Japanese → Russian
Gradient:  
low → medium  
→ smooth transition  
→ compatible field dynamics  
→ moderate accent difficulty

Japanese → French
Gradient:  
low → high  
→ discontinuous transition  
→ turbulent field dynamics  
→ high accent difficulty

This yields the MW‑prediction:

> For Japanese speakers, French should be significantly harder to approximate natively than Russian.

This prediction is:
- non‑cultural,  
- non‑psychological,  
- non‑essentialist,  
- and derived purely from field compatibility.

6. A more fine‑grained MW prediction

The MW architecture also predicts where the difficulty will manifest:

Japanese → Russian
- segmental difficulty: moderate  
- prosodic difficulty: high  
→ because Russian’s Δ is moderate but its prosody is complex

Japanese → French
- segmental difficulty: high  
- prosodic difficulty: high  
→ because French is high‑F, high‑Δ, low‑C across the board

This is a testable empirical claim.

7. Conclusion

The MW framework reframes accent difficulty as:

> a field‑transition problem, not a feature‑matching problem.

Languages differ in their F–Δ–C parameter regimes, and L2 acquisition requires moving from one attractor basin to another.  
Difficulty arises not from "distance" or "similarity", but from gradient incompatibility.

This approach explains:
- why typologically distant languages can be easy,  
- why typologically close languages can be hard,  
- why accent difficulty is asymmetric,  
- and why certain L2 accents cluster across speakers.

It also provides a unified account of the phonological convergence described in the previous section.
