
### Appendix 9 - The Prototaxites Hypothesis

Introduction: What Prototaxites Is — and What It Is Not

Prototaxites was a column‑shaped organism up to 8 m tall, occurring on land from the Middle Ordovician to the Devonian. It was long interpreted as a “giant fungus” or “lichen”; today its systematic position is considered incertae sedis: neither classical plant, nor animal, nor securely fungal. Recent work discusses it as a representative of an extinct eukaryotic lineage that cannot be assigned to any modern kingdom.

Building on this, one can formulate a consistent — though speculative — hypothesis that places Prototaxites functionally close to a kind of “terrestrial sponge.”

1. Morphology: Tubular Architecture and a “Pre‑Arboreal” Column

Observations:

- Column‑ or trunk‑like fossils up to 8 m tall, without leaves or branches.  
- Internally: thick tubular structures, fibrous bundles, net‑like tissues.

Interpretation:

This architecture can be read as a verticalized tube system: mechanical stability + material transport, without true wood.

Formal similarities exist with:

- sponge tissue (tubes, fiber bundles, modular structure),  
- “flexible columns” such as seagrasses or certain fungal fruiting bodies.

Hypothesis (Component 1):  
Prototaxites was a flexible, tube‑based giant organism that achieved height not through rigidity (wood), but through yielding, sponge‑like structural organization.

2. Modularity: Sponge Logic Instead of Organ Logic

Modern sponges:

- highly modular, “federal” organisms: loose cell associations, minimal fixed tissues, enormous cellular plasticity,  
- body plan: more network than hierarchical organism.

Applied to Prototaxites:

- absence of clear organs, tissues, or vascular bundles → compatible with a modular, non‑hierarchical body plan,  
- tubes + fiber bundles + lack of differentiation → functionally sponge‑like, even if not taxonomically proven.

Hypothesis (Component 2):  
Prototaxites may have been a highly modular multicellular organism whose organization resembled a sponge more than a plant — and was therefore evolutionarily extremely reconfigurable.

3. Biochemistry & Metabolic Space: Alternative but Stable Networks

Facts:

- Chemical signatures match neither classical fungi nor plants nor animals.  
- Isotope analyses indicate heterotrophic nutrition (i.e., “feeding” rather than photosynthesis).

Topological/metabolic reasoning:

Metabolic networks can be modeled as graphs/hypergraphs; only certain network topologies are stable and evolvable.

It is plausible that multiple stable “attractors” exist in metabolic space — not only those used by modern kingdoms.

Hypothesis (Component 3):  
Prototaxites may have used an alternative but stable metabolic attractor — biochemically different from modern fungi/animals, yet functionally comparable to a heterotrophic, sponge‑like land organism.

4. Ecology: Land, Wind, Moisture, and Flexibility

Silurian/Devonian environment:

- Open, largely treeless landscapes; low vegetation, spore‑based flora.  
- Likely higher average wind speeds, little surface roughness.  
- High humidity in many habitats, but no closed forests.

Functional fit:

- A flexible, tube‑based organism can dissipate wind pressure by bending rather than breaking.  
- Moisture‑dependent, modular structure fits open but not desiccated early terrestrial habitats.  
- As a heterotrophic “land sponge,” Prototaxites could have:
  - filtered organic material, microbial mats, soil solutions,  
  - absorbed nutrients from surface water and soil.

Hypothesis (Component 4):  
Prototaxites occupied the niche of a large, heterotrophic terrestrial filter‑feeder/decomposer that required moisture and open spaces — functionally analogous to a “sponge tree.”

5. Disappearance in the Late Devonian: A World Turning Hostile

Late Devonian:

- Emergence of the first true forests (e.g., Archaeopteris).  
- Strong CO₂ decline, global cooling, climatic instability.  
- Changing soils (more humus, stronger stratification), increasing surface dryness.

Consequences for a land sponge:

- Forests displaced open, moist habitats; water and nutrients became monopolized by root systems.  
- Drier, more seasonal conditions were maximally unfavorable for a moisture‑dependent, non‑woody giant organism.  
- Competition from woody plants with efficient water transport and mechanical stability.

Hypothesis (Component 5):  
The disappearance of Prototaxites in the Late Devonian reflects the collapse of a land‑sponge strategy overwhelmed by forests, dryness, and soil transformation.

6. Integrated Hypothesis: Prototaxites as a Terrestrial, Sponge‑Like Giant Organism

Combining all components yields a consistent, though speculative, hypothesis:

> Prototaxites was a large, highly modular, heterotrophic terrestrial organism with a tube‑based, sponge‑like architecture, occupying an early “tree role” in the open, moist landscapes of the Silurian/Devonian.  
> Biochemically, it used an alternative, stable metabolic attractor outside the classical plant/fungus/animal categories.  
> Its disappearance in the Late Devonian reflects the failure of this land‑sponge strategy in a world increasingly dominated by woody forests, dryness, and transformed soils.

Taxonomically it remains incertae sedis; functionally it is a land‑sponge hypothesis that unifies:

- observed morphology,  
- heterotrophy,  
- modularity,  
- and temporal disappearance  

into a single coherent narrative.

### Annex: Functional and Biochemical Parallels Between Sponges and Sea Cucumbers — and Their Relevance for Prototaxites

Although sponges (Porifera) and sea cucumbers (Holothuroidea) are phylogenetically extremely distant, they show remarkable convergent functional and biochemical properties relevant to Prototaxites once the organism is viewed not taxonomically but topologically‑functionally.

1. Functional Convergence: Tubes, Modularity, Hydraulic Logic

Sponges and sea cucumbers share structural principles that evolved independently but serve the same mechanical and ecological logic:

- Tube‑based architecture:  
  Sponges possess canal systems of various diameters; sea cucumbers have coelomic spaces and a water‑vascular system. Both use internal tubes for stabilization and material distribution.

- Flexibility instead of rigid skeletons:  
  Sponges use spongin or loose silicate spicules; sea cucumbers use mutable collagen matrices. Both rely on compliance rather than lignified rigidity.

- Modularity and redundancy:  
  Sponges are true cell federations; sea cucumbers have segmented functional systems. Both are robust to local damage and allow reconfiguration without systemic collapse.

This functional proximity is crucial because Prototaxites also shows a tube‑based, flexible, modular columnar structure that is neither plant‑like nor fungal.

2. Biochemical Topology: Alternative Support Polymers

The biochemical distance between sponges and sea cucumbers is large, but their support tissues share key properties:

- Neither uses plant polymers like cellulose or lignin.  
- Neither uses fungal chitin walls.  
- Both employ aromatic and phenolic modifications to stabilize tissues.  
- Both possess non‑mineralized, organic support structures that remain flexible.

The chemical signature of Prototaxites — a mixture of aliphatic, aromatic, and phenolic compounds, but no chitin and no true lignin — fits precisely into this space of alternative, non‑plant, non‑fungal support polymers.

Topologically, Prototaxites lies near the functional space occupied by sponges and sea cucumbers:  
a space where aromatic polymers, tube mechanics, and modular organization are mutually compatible.

3. Metabolic Space Topology: Stable Attractors Beyond Classical Kingdoms

Modern systems biology shows that metabolic networks can be understood as dynamical systems with stable attractors. Sponges and sea cucumbers, despite their distance, occupy robust, low‑energy attractors that:

- are modular,  
- allow alternative support polymers,  
- require little specialization,  
- and permit high reconfigurability.

A hypothetical terrestrial sponge‑relative could occupy its own attractor that:

- is heterotrophic,  
- uses tube‑based mechanics,  
- employs aromatic polymers,  
- remains modular,  
- but shares neither animal, plant, nor fungal signatures.

The chemical and structural data on Prototaxites fit exactly into such an alternative attractor.

4. Relevance for the Hypothesis

The functional and biochemical parallels between sponges and sea cucumbers show that:

- tube architecture,  
- flexible support polymers,  
- modular organization,  
- and heterotrophic lifestyles  

are not exclusive to any particular animal lineage, but are topological solutions that can arise in different metabolic spaces.

This makes it plausible that Prototaxites:

- was not an animal,  
- but occupied a functional space using sponge‑like principles,  
- without sharing their biochemistry or genetics.

It would thus represent an independent, extinct eukaryotic lineage that arrived at the same topological solutions as sponges — but in a completely different biochemical attractor.
