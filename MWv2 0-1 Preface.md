### Preface

We live in a time in which our concepts, models, and disciplines drift further and further apart.  
We possess an abundance of tools, yet no shared coordinate system.  
This book does not attempt to add another tool.  
It attempts to reveal the structure beneath all of them - a structure we intuitively know, but have culturally forgotten how to see.

This is not a revival of structuralism or any of its later variants.  
Although it uses terms like “structure” and “relation”, the framework developed here is dynamic, non‑hierarchical, and processual — closer to neutral monism and topology than to any structuralist framework.

---

*A note on reading this book*

Readers familiar with Bertrand Russell’s The Analysis of Mind or Luc Ciompi’s The Emotional Foundations of Thinking (affect‑logics) may recognize certain resonances.  
This book does not follow either framework, but it shares their intuition that mind, meaning, and world are not built from objects, but from dynamic fields of relation.

---

*Why this framework is needed*

The metaphoric world brings into view a foundational assumption so deeply embedded in our culture that it rarely appears as an assumption at all: the belief that our epistemology is complete - that we know how knowledge works, that perception, language, and logic are settled matters.  
But once one begins to think in flows, fields, and resonances, this certainty dissolves.  
The metaphoric world exposes several silent premises that have shaped our culture for centuries - premises that no longer hold when viewed through a semantic‑topological lens.

Many of the tensions of the modern world arise from epistemic habits shaped for a simpler age - habits that can no longer carry the weight of the systems they are meant to understand. The Metaphoric World offers a descriptive, universal, and generative framework for sense‑making that matches the complexity of the worlds we now inhabit.

1. Knowledge as a finished architecture  
2. The world consists of things  
3. Meaning arises in the mind  
4. Technology is artificial, nature is real  
5. Linearity is normal, cyclicity is exceptional  
6. Dignity is a metaphysical attribute  
7. Systems are machines  
8. Models must be reductionist  

These assumptions form the invisible architecture of our present.  
The semantic‑topological framework does not simply challenge them - it shows that they are local projections, useful but not fundamental.

*How to navigate the book*

Section I begins with concrete phenomena such as color and the limits of linear logic.  
Section II introduces the basic field operations - distinction, metaphor, and the triadic structure.  
Section III shows how meaning and language emerge from these dynamics.  
Section IV extends the framework to entities, perception, and world‑formation.  
Later sections explore society, culture, and the dynamics of collective fields.

The framework itself is simple.  
It describes something accessible to every human being - from the infant who relates to the world through presence and resonance, to the political decision‑maker navigating intricate systems.  
If it appears “complicated”, that is a symptom of our time: we live in a culture so far removed from its own cognitive foundations that simple structures must be expressed through abstract terminology.

The terminology is not a barrier but a guide.  
It points toward patterns already familiar in experience, even if rarely named explicitly.  
Whenever the text becomes abstract, it does so only to illuminate something simple that our culture has learned to overlook.

The framework is scientifically interoperable: every part of it aligns with contemporary research across physics, cognitive science, linguistics, systems theory, and mathematics. It is also practically applicable - not as a doctrine, but as a generative foundation for sense‑making that can reorganize concepts, institutions, and collective fields without flattening their differences.

This is a text about scientific reasoning, but equally about anthropology, meaning, language, perception, and cognition.  
It moves between physics and philosophy, between culture and mathematics, without belonging to any of these domains - because what is described here is not a new discipline, but a shared structure that becomes visible once these domains are viewed through a single lens.


I did not invent this structure.  
I merely uncovered it - as someone who has spent long enough at the edges of different fields to see where their lines intersect.

*A brief note on perspective*

I write from within the German cultural sphere, and traces of that background will be visible in tone and emphasis.  
Yet the framework itself is not culturally bound.  
Its structure is cross‑cultural without being flattening, capable of resonating with different intellectual traditions without reducing them to a single viewpoint.

*About this version*

This book grew out of the modular v1.0 release, which presented the ideas in raw form as a non‑linear conceptual field.  
The present version is a transformation of the first one.
What was exploratory in v1.0 becomes structural here; what was modular becomes integrated; what was intuitive becomes formal.  
The major conceptual addition is the form‑dynamics of the Basis‑3 field (27 form‑regions and their transitions), which was not yet present in v1.0 and now serves as the backbone of the entire architecture.
