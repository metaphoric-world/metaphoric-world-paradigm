### Chapter 19 - Causality and Time as Local Field‑Order

Orientation: Why This Chapter Matters

Causality and time are the last two pillars of the linear worldview.  
They survived the collapse of substance metaphysics, the dissolution of absolute space, the dethroning of the Cartesian subject, and the rise of relational physics.  
Even after quantum theory, relativity, and dynamical systems, most people still imagine the world as a sequence of events unfolding along a single temporal axis, linked by causal arrows.

This chapter does not claim that the universe behaves differently than physics describes.  
It shows that the Metaphoric World allows us to understand causality and time in a way that is structurally richer, historically grounded, and cognitively more accurate than the linear models we inherited.

Section II traced the rise of Basis‑2 dominance: the long drift from relational, metaphorical, triadic world‑formation into a binary, sequential, algorithmic worldview.  
Section IV showed how the concept of a “thing” emerged from this drift — a stable entity carved out of a relational field.

Causality and time are conceptual residues of this same historical trajectory.  
They are not fundamental features of the world, but projections of particular ordering modes onto a deeper, non‑linear field.

### 19.1 - The Historical Line: How Causality Became Linear

Early cosmologies were relational. They explained the world through correspondences, resonances, and analogical structures.  
A storm was not “caused” by a pressure gradient; it was like a disturbance in the harmony of the elements. These were not errors but attempts to map patterns onto patterns - metaphors as field‑operations.

The linear concept of causality required a long conceptual compression:
- Greek atomism: discrete units replacing relational continua  
- Scholastic logic: binary inference replacing analogical reasoning  
- Cartesian mechanism: the world as a machine of pushes and pulls  
- Newtonian determinism: time as a universal axis, causality as force  
- Industrial algorithmisation: processes as sequences  
- Digital computation: the world as a chain of binary operations  

Once the world was imagined as discrete things moving through a universal time, causality became the glue that held the sequence together.

But as Section II showed, events are not fundamental. They are distinctions in a field - not points in a metaphysical timeline.

### 19.2 - The Problem of Causality in Modern Science

Despite its cultural dominance, causality has no stable scientific definition.

Physics does not define causality

Modern physics uses:
- symmetries  
- conservation laws  
- constraints  
- variational principles  
- dynamical equations  

None of these contain causal arrows.  
They describe coherence, not “A causes B.”

Relativity provides light‑cone structure, not metaphysical necessity.  
Quantum mechanics avoids causal language entirely.  
Statistical physics derives the arrow of time from entropy, not from cause.

Philosophy of physics is divided
- Humeanism: causality is a pattern we project  
- Neo‑Russellianism: causality is not fundamental  
- Interventionism: causality defined by manipulation  
- Constraint‑based views: “causes” are structural limitations  

Cognitive science: humans are causal cognizers

Humans think in causal terms because it is a useful cognitive shortcut - a Basis 2 projection on a non-linear field. Causality is a mental model, not an ontological feature.

The scientific meta‑consensus:

> Causality is not fundamental.  
> It is an emergent, model‑dependent, cognitively useful pattern.

### 19.3 - Causality as Local Field‑Order

In a semantic‑topological field, there are no causal arrows. There are:
- K — coherence  
- Δ — uncertainty radius  
- A — associative structure  
- C₁/C₂ — contextual embedding  

Causality appears when a region of the field becomes locally stable:
- Δ becomes small  
- A‑lines align  
- K becomes high  
- C₁/C₂ remain stable  

A “cause” is simply:

> a region of the field whose coherence persists long enough to be interpreted as directional.

Causality is not a force. It is a gradient of coherence.
When coherence is high and uncertainty is low, the field behaves as if one thing leads to another.  
When coherence breaks down, causality dissolves.

### 19.4 - Physical Time as Linear Projection (Basis‑2)

Time is not a dimension of the field.  
It is a projection - a way of imposing linear order on a non‑linear continuum.

Physical time arises when a system introduces:
- periodicity  
- clocks  
- repetition  
- metric sequences  
- algorithmic ordering  

This is a Basis‑2 operation: a digital, uniform, sequential mode of structuring.

Physical time appears:
- measurable  
- homogeneous  
- directional  
- stable  

But this appearance is the result of a projection.  
The field itself is not temporal.

Relativity confirms this: time is not absolute; it is a local ordering mode that bends with the field.

### 19.5 - Semantic Time as Resonant Structuring (Basis‑3)

Semantic time arises when consciousness structures the field through:
- distinction  
- meaning activation  
- affective coupling  
- recursive reflection  
- metaphorical layering  

This is Basis‑3 time - resonant, curved, subjective.

Semantic time:
- stretches in boredom  
- collapses in flow

- folds in memory  
- condenses in meaning  

It is not a sequence but a pattern of coherence.

Semantic time is the internal dynamics of a structured field.

### 19.6 - Why Physical and Semantic Time Seem Incompatible

Because they are different projections of the same field:

- physical time = linear ordering  
- semantic time = resonant structuring  

They are not two kinds of time.  
They are two ordering modes.

Their incompatibility is a topological effect:
- linear projection vs. curved field  
- metric sequence vs. semantic resonance  
- uniformity vs. affective modulation  

Gödel's rotating universe solution to Einstein's field equations makes this concrete: it demonstrates that closed timelike curves are mathematically consistent with general relativity, which means linear temporal order is not a structural necessity of the field equations but a property of particular local solutions. Time, like causality, is a local coherence mode - not a global feature of the field.

### 19.7 - The Role of c as a Boundary of Local Ordering

Physics encodes the boundary of local temporal order in the constant c.  
In this chapter, c is mentioned only in this minimal, standard physical sense:

- c defines the maximum rate at which local coherence can propagate  
- c marks the limit of linear temporal projection  
- c separates local order from non‑local structure  

This is not a metaphysical claim, and certainly not a form of quantum mysticism.  
It is simply the way modern physics formalizes the boundary of local ordering.

The deeper structural role of c - and why it appears as a boundary in both physical and semantic dynamics - will be explained carefully and non‑mystically in Chapter 20.

> Both are projections, not properties.

Causality is a coherence gradient.  
Time is an ordering mode.  
Both are tools - not metaphysical structures.
